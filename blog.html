Friday, October 4, 2013

Of all the weekdays, at least for this semester, Friday is my favorite. Since my classes start at 12pm on Fridays, I'm able to sleep in, not attend a class about insects, not take a math quiz, and eat a nice unhurried lunch at Crossroads. Some of my peers won't agree with my last statement, but I'll say, today's catfish was gooood.

Math lecture today was taught by another GSI. I learned today that Professor Graunbaum missed the past week because of a leg injury. For this lecture, the GSI introduced pretty cool real world applications of linear algebra. But, after that, I kinda zoned out - I couldn't really understand his writing or his lecturing. Instead, I continued to finish the math hw for the current section, continuing on my plan to be a day ahead of lecture instead of playing catch-up each time. It really helps - before going to discussion, I already have a basic understanding of the section and, thus, the discussion reinforces the knowledge I gained from reading ahead.

By far, the most interesting part of my day was visiting an EECS lab with my friend Daniel Pok. He introduced me to a graduate student, Victor, who led us into his lab. In the lab, a behemouth $100k full motion driving simulator stood in the center, along with other humanoid robots off to the side. Victor explained that the simulator is going to be used for observing how people perform under duress, drunkeness, etc. For now, though, he says that they installed Need for Speed on the simulator. I watched Daniel use the simulator first and, from my vantage point, every time he crashed, the simulator jerked up and down and side to side. After another person used the simulator, I got to play! Compared to the previous drivers, I chose to drive more cautiously around curves. It felt extremely smooth when around banks the chair rotated around the center. And, surprisingly, when I crashed, the jerk, yaw, and rotation wasn't as sharp as it looked from the outside. When my car finally came to a halt, due to the extensive damage to my car, I was on my second lap, the only one in the group to do so. =D It was definitely an epic ending to a long long week! K. I'm exhausted.

Wednesday, October 2, 2013

Lately, I realized that I needed a forum to rant... nah, churn out some thoughts and ideas. Perhaps, it would also be good to have a journal of the events that happen throughout college - it'd be interesting to look back on my successes, failures, interesting happenings, and what-nots. At this point, I'll admit that my writing lacks the eloquence of writers such as Tim O'Brien and Joseph Heller. Even so, I doubt my life would resemble a James Bond, or Jason Bourne, action movie. (I like both.) So, excuse my cacophonic writing. Please.

For the past few days, I've been troubled by two problems. The first lies in academics. It appears that my many attempts to conquer my important classes, namely Math 54 and CS61B, have been futile. In Math 54, I flunked the first midterm. Turns out studying from the past midterms was a mistake - the questions on the actual midterm were more difficult and more comprehensive. To address this, I resolved to writing down the problems on the homework such that reviewing the homework is effortless. So far, it appears to be working, although I did miss a question on the quiz today. I incorrectly made an assumption that a matrix was supposed to be diagonalizable, and, thus, my proof was too narrow. As for CS61B, I'm falling behind - got a lot of catchup to do.

My second problem lies with my social abilities. Recently, I have been failing to muster the energy to engage in a meaningful conversation. Yesterday, my friend Kenny remarked after observing me with some floormates at Crossroads that I barely spoke a word. Today, I felt invisible as I passed by a floormate without any acknowledgement. I need to figure out how to make a nonacademic conversation, but Kenny warns that I might be too calculated in this approach. I need to experiment, be spontaneous, and take risks. Undoubtedly, this will be one of my biggest challenges in college.

Nevertheless, I hope you enjoyed my honesty in this blog? Is that what you call it? Many have viewed me as an infallible and ultra-smart student. In reality, I feel like I'm being torn to shreds at this point. Maybe writing this will alleviate things. At the very least, I can keep track of my progress. In other news, I'm still playing around with a newly acquired Raspberry Pi and, today, I had an interesting lecture by Professor Schruben in my IEOR seminar. He talked about how operations research was applied during his time in the military during the Vietnam War and also in other companies he worked for. Fascinating.
